VASCULOPATIA DA ALLOTRAPIANTO CARDIACO E DISLIPIDEMIA: STUDIO RETROSPETTIVO - THE CAVALIER STUDY

ABSTRACT

INTRODUZIONE

Il trapianto cardiaco è il trattamento di scelta nell’insufficienza cardiaca avanzata. La vasculopatia coronarica dell’allotrapianto (CAV) rappresenta una delle principali cause di morbilità e mortalità a lungo termine. Scopo dello studio è stato valutare l’associazione tra dislipidemia e sviluppo di CAV, controllando per altri fattori di rischio.

METODOLOGIA

In questo studio retrospettivo monocentrico sono stati analizzati i dati di 133 pazienti sottoposti a trapianto cardiaco presso l’ASST Grande Ospedale Metropolitano Niguarda di Milano (2010–2018). La diagnosi di CAV è stata posta secondo le linee guida ISHLT 2010 mediante coronarografia e valutazione funzionale del graft a 1 e 5 anni.

RISULTATI

A 5 anni, il 31% dei pazienti ha sviluppato CAV (15% CAV1, 10% CAV2, 6% CAV3). Non si sono riscontrate differenze tra i gruppi per età al trapianto, eziologia dello scompenso, supporto al circolo o tempi di attesa in lista trapianto. I pazienti con CAV erano più frequentemente uomini (74% vs 54%, p=0.025), con maggiore esposizione al fumo (45% vs 20%, p=0.001), donatori più anziani (46±13 vs 39±14 anni, p=0.003) e livelli di LDL tendenzialmente più elevati a un anno (113±29 vs 101±30 mg/dl, p=0.055). All’analisi univariata, età del donatore (HR 1.04; p=0.003) ed esposizione al fumo (HR 1.62; p=0.047) risultavano predittori significativi di CAV. Al modello multivariato, solo l’età del donatore manteneva una significativa associazione con il rischio di CAV (p=0.006); il fumo mostrava una tendenza (p=0.052), mentre i livelli di LDL non erano associati (p=0.390).

CONCLUSIONI

Nel nostro studio, l’età del donatore è risultata un fattore indipendente associato allo sviluppo di CAV a 5 anni dal trapianto cardiaco. I livelli di LDL ad un anno dal trapianto, pur mostrando una tendenza nell’analisi univariata, non sono risultati predittivi di CAV al modello multivariato.

CARDIAC ALLOTRANSPLANT VASCULOPATHY AND DYSLIPIDAEMIA: A RETROSPECTIVE STUDY

INTRODUCTION

Development of CAV is triggered by a variety of risk factors. Immune and non-immune features converge on a common pathway of coronary endothelial inflammation. An impaired and exaggerated response to vascular injury follows, involving smooth muscle proliferation, lipid deposition, and accumulation of inflammatory cells, causing intimal thickening and fibrosis1,2.In contrast to the focal and eccentric epicardial lesions in atherosclerosis, CAV is diffuse and affects epicardial and intramural vessels3. CAV is the leading long-term cause of death and re-transplantation in Heart Transplant population4–6. Moreover, the cumulative morbidity rate of CAV increases over time, developing in 30% of patients at 5 years and in almost 50% of patients at 10 years7.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study description

We conducted a single-center, retrospective study of consecutive patients who underwent heart transplantation between January 1, 2010, and December 31, 2019. Patients were considered eligible if they were aged 18 years or older at the time of transplantation, had lipid profiles assessed using a standard lipid panel, and underwent surveillance coronary angiography at both 1 and 5 years after transplantation for the evaluation of cardiac allograft vasculopathy (CAV). Remnant cholesterol was calculated using the formula: total cholesterol − HDL cholesterol − LDL cholesterol, with all values expressed in mg/dL.

Baseline data

Baseline demographic information for donors and recipients was obtained from the electronic medical record and the National Transplant Center registry. Echocardiographic, angiographic, and medication data were extracted from evaluations performed during annual follow-up visits. Laboratory results were collected from the electronic medical record, with baseline lipid and laboratory values defined as the closest available measurements prior to transplantation. Lipid assessments were performed using standard assays.

Coronary angiography

Coronary angiography was performed using standard projections of the right and left coronary arteries. Per protocol, angiographic evaluations were scheduled at 1 year and 5 years after transplantation, unless declined by the patient or deferred due to relative contraindications such as renal insufficiency, contrast allergy, or vascular access limitations. Quantitative coronary angiography included assessment of luminal narrowing, lesion location, and vessel involvement. Cardiac Allograft Vasculopathy (CAV) was graded according to the 2010 International Society for Heart and Lung Transplantation (ISHLT) classification.

Patients were stratified into two groups based on the presence of CAV on the invasive coronary angiography performed on the 5th year after heart transplantation. The non-CAV group corresponded to ISHLT CAV0 and included patients without angiographically detectable lesions. The CAV group encompassed all patients with any angiographically evident stenosis (≥ISHLT CAV1), regardless of graft function, including those classified as CAV1, CAV2, or CAV3.

Statistical analysis

Categorical variables were summarized as absolute numbers with corresponding percentages and compared using the chi-square test, Fisher’s exact test, or the Kruskal–Wallis test, as appropriate. Continuous variables were expressed as mean ± standard deviation or median with interquartile range and analyzed with either the two-sample Student’s t-test or the Wilcoxon rank-sum test, depending on data distribution. A two-sided P value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical analyses were conducted in R. Associations between lipid concentrations (mg/dL) and measures of cardiac allograft vasculopathy (CAV) were examined using logistic regression models, adjusted for prespecified baseline and follow-up covariates.

RESULTS

Clinical features of transplant recipients

At the time of transplant, recipients had a mean age of 46 years (SD: 14), with patients who later developed CAV being older at 51 years compared to 45 years in those without CAV Table 1. Overall, 61% of the cohort were men, though male sex was more common among patients with CAV (76% vs 54%). Blood group distribution was balanced, with group O (42%) and group A (37%) predominating. The leading causes of heart failure were dilated cardiomyopathy (37%) and ischemic cardiomyopathy (29%), the latter being more frequent among patients with CAV (44% vs 22%). Traditional cardiovascular risk factors were variably represented: arterial hypertension (28%), dyslipidemia (25%), diabetes mellitus (14%), and prior smoking history (30%), the latter showing a strong association with CAV (49% vs 20%). Median time on the waiting list was 13 months, and among patients requiring mechanical circulatory support, short-term devices were predominant (87%), whereas long-term support was used in only 13% of cases.Donors were on average 41 years old (SD: 14), with those associated with CAV being significantly older (48 vs 39 years). The majority were male (61%), and stroke was the leading cause of death (50%). Median ischemic time was 184 minutes, and donor coronary angiography was available in 46% of cases, with a tendency toward higher prevalence among CAV recipients (67% vs 38%).

Lipids

Overall, median atherogenic lipid levels increased early after transplant and gradually declined over time. At baseline, median LDL-C was 86 mg/dL, triglycerides 92 mg/dL, and remnant cholesterol 18 mg/dL, with 26.5% of patients achieving LDL-C < 70 mg/dL (Table 2). LDL-C peaked at 6 months (118 mg/dL), alongside triglycerides (150 mg/dL) and remnant cholesterol (29.5 mg/dL), while only 5.7% of patients remained below the LDL-C target. By 1 year, LDL-C, triglycerides, and remnant cholesterol decreased to 98.5, 129.5, and 26 mg/dL, respectively, and 6.8% of patients maintained LDL-C < 70 mg/dL. At 5 years, median LDL-C, triglycerides, and remnant cholesterol were 91.5, 115, and 21 mg/dL, with 15.3% of patients below the LDL-C threshold. These data indicate that atherogenic lipids peak in the early post-transplant period but decline over long-term follow-up, with only a minority achieving optimal LDL-C throughout(Figure 1).

Cardiac Allograft Vasculopathy

One year after transplantation, most patients showed no evidence of CAV. Over the subsequent four years, the proportion of patients developing CAV steadily increased, and by five years post-transplant, overall prevalence had risen from 11.7% to 30.8% (Table 3). This progression was observed across all severity levels, highlighting the gradual development of CAV over time and the shift toward more advanced stages.

Univariate logistic regression

In univariable logistic regression analyses, several recipient and donor characteristics were significantly associated with the development of any type of CAV at 5 year post transplant (Table 4). Older recipient age was associated with higher odds of CAV (OR 1.04 per year, 95% CI 1.01–1.07, p = 0.022), while female sex was protective compared with males (OR 0.38, 95% CI 0.16–0.85, p = 0.023). Higher donor age also increased the risk of CAV (OR 1.05 per year, 95% CI 1.02–1.09, p = 0.001). Among lifestyle factors, a history of smoking was strongly associated with higher odds of developing CAV (OR 3.69, 95% CI 1.63–8.53, p = 0.002). LDL levels at 1 year showed a trend toward higher odds of CAV (OR 1.01 per mg/dL, 95% CI 1.00–1.03, p = 0.085), although this did not reach statistical significance.

Multivariate logistic regression

In multivariable logistic regression(Table 5), donor age was the only significant independent predictor of any type of cardiac allograft vasculopathy (CAV) at 5 year post-transplant (OR 1.05 per year, 95% CI 1.02–1.10, p = 0.006). Recipient age, sex, LDL at 1 year, and history of smoking were not statistically significant, although smoking showed a trend toward higher risk (OR 2.50, 95% CI 0.95–6.70, p = 0.064). These variables were selected based on clinical relevance and univariable associations, with LDL included to explore the potential impact of post-transplant lipid levels. Overall, the analysis highlights donor age as the strongest independent predictor of CAV in this cohort.

DISCUSSION

Identification of recipients at risk of CAV is difficult, due to the large number of risk factors associated with its development. Overall, data from literature support a model in which CAV is a manifestation of multiple risk factors, including pathophysiological features related to the donor, as well as metabolic and immunologic traits related to the recipient. Dyslipidaemia is common and contributes to the risk of disease. Statins are the cornerstone of antilipemic therapy in heart transplant patients. These drugs may prolong survival, reduce the incidence of CAV, and potentially induce regression of pre-existing atherosclerotic lesions. Unfortunately, statins have the potential to induce significant drug interactions with common post­transplant therapies. Since the denervated transplanted heart prevents recipients from experiencing pain, the early detection of CAV is of utmost importance. However, in clinical practice, routine surveillance is performed via coronary angiography. These invasive techniques require technical expertise, and may not be advisable in all patients due to anatomic unsuitability or chronic kidney disease.
